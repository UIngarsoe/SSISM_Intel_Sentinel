## Executive Summary

On 20 April 2026, Senior General **0** announced a detailed “100 Days Priority Plan” for the new administration. The plan lists agricultural loans, infrastructure upgrades, communications expansion, education reform, health initiatives, and disaster recovery.

At surface level, the agenda appears practical and development-oriented. Under closer examination, it functions as a narrative deflection strategy: a list of technical and economic projects presented as evidence of “normal governance,” while the structural causes of Myanmar’s crisis remain unaddressed.

**SSISM assessment:** the plan is not a roadmap for recovery. It is a roadmap for delay. Development deliverables are being used to substitute for political legitimacy, reconciliation, and consent.

---

## 1. What the 100 Days Plan Actually Promises

The announcement covers several sectors:

- **Agriculture & Rural Economy**: Expanded crop loans and farm machinery financing.
- **Infrastructure**: Airport upgrades, warehouses, railway repairs, road and bridge works.
- **Communications**: New mobile towers and 4G LTE expansion.
- **Education**: Technical subjects in schools, selected universities upgraded to research status, pilot “Smart School” programs.
- **Health**: New township health administrators, nursing school upgrades, foreign medical cooperation.
- **Disaster Recovery**: Reconstruction of public infrastructure damaged by disasters and conflict.

The framing emphasises service delivery, unity, and development in underdeveloped regions.

---

## 2. What the Plan Deliberately Omits — The Actual Drivers of Crisis

The plan is silent on the issues defining Myanmar’s present condition:

- Political legitimacy after the 2021 coup.
- Ongoing armed conflict and fragmented territorial control.
- Mass displacement of civilians and humanitarian access.
- Continued detention of elected leaders and political prisoners.
- Collapse of public trust in state institutions.
- Restrictions on media, civil society, and political participation.

By limiting discussion to technical projects, the regime reframes a political and constitutional crisis as a development management problem.

This is not governance reform. It is crisis displacement through administrative language.

---

## 3. Narrative Strategy: Development as Legitimacy Laundering

The structure of the plan reflects a recognisable communication pattern:

- **Technocratic vocabulary** to project modern governance.
- **Short-term deliverables** to create visible signs of progress.
- **Removal of political context** from public messaging.

This follows the broader “New Chapter” narrative that presents administrative activity as proof of stability. In SSISM terms, this is **Development Theatre** — visible projects masking the absence of political settlement.

---

## 4. Why These Measures Cannot Resolve the Underlying Crisis

Even if executed fully, these initiatives cannot address core instability:

- Loans cannot stabilise farmers in conflict zones.
- Infrastructure cannot function normally across contested territories.
- Education reform cannot succeed amid displacement and militarisation of youth.
- Health improvements cannot offset the effects of ongoing violence and coercion.

Sustainable recovery requires political inclusion, trust, and legitimate governance structures. Without these foundations, development efforts remain temporary and fragile.

---

## 5. SSISM Analytical Assessment

**Strengths (from regime perspective)**

- Generates short-term positive optics.
- Appeals to rural economic concerns.
- Provides neutral talking points for external engagement.

**Weaknesses and Risks**

- Avoids the root causes of instability.
- Reinforces public scepticism by ignoring political realities.
- Risks credibility loss if conflict continues while projects are advertised.

**Overall Effectiveness**

Low. The plan is a sophisticated distraction but cannot substitute for political reconciliation or legitimacy.

---

## Conclusion

The 100 Days Priority Plan does not attempt to resolve Myanmar’s crisis. It attempts to reframe it.

Technical projects are presented as evidence of progress while the political foundations of instability remain unaddressed. Roads, loans, and schools cannot replace consent, inclusion, and reconciliation.

Until political legitimacy is confronted directly, development initiatives function primarily as delay mechanisms rather than recovery pathways.
