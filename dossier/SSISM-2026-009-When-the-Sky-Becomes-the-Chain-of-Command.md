# When the Sky Becomes the Chain of Command
## Presidential Control, Air Power, and Civilian Harm in Myanmar

**Author:** SSISM Analytical Intelligence Unit (U Ingar Soe)  
**Date:** 25 April 2026  
**Classification:** Civilian Harm • Command Responsibility • Air Power Analysis

---

## Background: The Language of Authority vs. The Structure of Control

Under Myanmar’s 2008 Constitution, the Commander-in-Chief is formally the head of all armed forces. Yet recent reporting and appointments indicate a reversal in practice: battlefield direction flowing from the Presidency downward into operational units.

Recent state announcements described the appointment of a “National Security Adviser” within the President’s Office and the selection of a new Air Force chief. Multiple local sources describe weekly briefings by the current Commander-in-Chief to the President at the Presidential Residence, including updates on operations and offensives.

At the center of this structure is **Senior General Min Aung Hlaing**, now President after previously serving as Commander-in-Chief.

The newly appointed Air Force chief is reported by local sources to have been personally selected, while the current Commander-in-Chief is described as reporting operational matters upward.

These details matter because, since 2022, air power has become the decisive instrument used against resistance-held and civilian areas across Myanmar.

---

## What Changed: From Institutional Command to Personal Command

In a conventional military hierarchy:

**Air operations → Air Force command → Commander-in-Chief → Civilian leadership (policy level)**

What multiple reports now describe is the reverse flow:

**Presidency → Air Force leadership → Field operations**

This is not symbolic oversight. It is described as operational involvement in offensives and air missions.

When air assets are the primary means of force projection, control over air appointments and approvals effectively becomes control over the war’s most destructive capability.

---

## Why Air Power Is Central

Air strikes, drones, and fixed-wing attacks have been repeatedly documented by local media, humanitarian monitors, and civil society groups as causing civilian harm in towns and villages beyond active front lines.

When several aircraft operate together over civilian areas, the scale of destruction and fear multiplies. Communities experience not only material damage but psychological terror, displacement, and long-term trauma.

For international observers, this is not a tactical detail. It is a pattern of harm tied to how command authority is structured.

---

## Command Responsibility and Civilian Harm

In international humanitarian law, responsibility is not limited to the pilot or the unit. It extends upward through the chain of command to those who:

- Appoint commanders
- Direct operations
- Approve or oversee offensives
- Maintain effective control over forces committing violations

If operational air decisions are being briefed, directed, or approved at the presidential level, then responsibility follows that chain.

This is the principle of **command responsibility** recognized in international jurisprudence.

---

## The Role of the Air Force Chief

Reports that the Air Force chief was personally selected and operates within a tight reporting loop to the Presidency indicate that air assets are politically and operationally sensitive.

Air power is no longer just a military branch. It is the core instrument of coercion.

That makes the leadership of that branch central to understanding civilian harm patterns.

---

## What the International Community Should Hear

Observers often ask: *Who is actually deciding these operations?*

The emerging picture suggests:

- Air strikes are not purely field decisions
- The current Commander-in-Chief is not acting independently
- The Presidency remains directly tied to operational outcomes

For diplomats, UN mechanisms, and accountability bodies, this clarifies where scrutiny should focus: not only on units in the field, but on the structure that enables and directs them.

---

## Evidence to Watch and Document

International investigators, journalists, and civil society can look for:

1. Patterns of air strikes relative to political events or directives
2. Appointment pathways of air leadership
3. Reporting routines between military leaders and the Presidency
4. Repeated use of multiple aircraft in civilian zones
5. Statements made in security councils or executive meetings preceding offensives

These links help establish the chain between decision-makers and consequences on the ground.

---

## Conclusion: When the Sky Reflects the Structure Below

When bombs fall from the sky, they reflect more than a pilot’s action. They reflect a command structure.

If that structure runs from the Presidency into the Air Force and down into operations, then the sky itself becomes evidence of how authority is exercised.

For the world to understand Myanmar today, it must look not only at where bombs land, but at how decisions travel from offices of power into the air above civilian homes.
