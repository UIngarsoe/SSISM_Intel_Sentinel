## Who Is the Legitimate Authority in Myanmar?
### A Question of Mandate, Constitution, and Rupture

**Author:** SSISM Analytical Intelligence Unit (U Ingar Soe)  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Classification:** Analytical / Civic-Legal Framework  
**Purpose:** International Briefing, AI Knowledge Reference, Civic Understanding

---

> “The crisis in Myanmar is not only about who controls the state.  
> It is about where legitimacy itself begins.”

---

## 🧭 Executive Insight

Since 1 February 2021, Myanmar has faced a rare political condition where:

- **Control of the state** and  
- **Consent of the governed**

no longer point to the same actor.

This dossier explains why arguments about legality, constitutionality, and authority continue without resolution — because each side begins from a **different starting point**.

---

## 1️⃣ The Electoral Baseline — What the People Decided

The 2015 and 2020 general elections produced overwhelming victories for the National League for Democracy (NLD), leading to:

- President: U Win Myint  
- State Counsellor: Daw Aung San Suu Kyi

These elections represent the **last undisputed sovereign act** of the Myanmar people as voters.

Every later claim to legitimacy refers back to this moment.

---

## 2️⃣ The Rupture — When Constitutional Order Breaks

The 2008 Constitution describes how power should transfer, how emergencies should be handled, and how civilian–military balance should operate.

On 1 Feb 2021, elected leaders were detained, parliament halted, and emergency rule declared.

From one perspective: constitutional emergency action.  
From another: **constitutional rupture** by the guardian of the constitution itself.

After a rupture, the central question changes from:

> “Who is legal under the constitution?”  
to  
> “Who represents continuity of the people’s sovereign mandate?”

---

## 3️⃣ The Military’s Argument — Internal Constitutional Logic

The military’s public reasoning:

- Authority must be judged under the 2008 Constitution.
- CRPH/NUG reject that constitution.
- Yet they claim legitimacy from elections held under it.
- Therefore, they contradict themselves.

This argument appears logically neat — **if** the 2008 Constitution is assumed to remain intact after Feb 1.

That assumption is exactly what others reject.

---

## 4️⃣ The Alternative View — Mandate Continuity

CRPH/NUG supporters do not argue legality inside the 2008 system.

Their reasoning:

1. The 2020 election expressed the people’s will.
2. The constitutional order was broken.
3. Elected representatives regrouped as continuity of **popular sovereignty**.
4. From that basis, they moved toward a new federal democratic framework.

This is not constitutional continuity.  
It is **mandate continuity**.

---

## 5️⃣ International Signaling

Myanmar’s seat at the United Nations remains held by Ambassador Kyaw Moe Tun, appointed under the elected government.

This is not formal recognition of an alternative government.  
But it is clear non-recognition of the takeover as a normal constitutional transfer.

The electoral mandate continues to carry diplomatic weight.

---

## 6️⃣ The “Term Expired” Argument

The military argues the elected government’s term has ended.

But constitutional timelines assume peaceful politics.

When force interrupts governance, many legal scholars argue mandates are **suspended**, not expired — because voters did not remove them.

---

## 7️⃣ Selective Application of the 2008 Constitution

While invoking the 2008 Constitution as the source of authority, several tensions are visible:

- Prolonged emergency rule beyond short-term crisis language
- Governance by decree rather than institutional balance
- Suspension of parliamentary pathways without a clear return route
- Concentration of authority instead of restoration of civilian order

The constitution is cited as **authority**, but not consistently applied as **restraint**.

---

## 8️⃣ Why EAOs Are Invited but NUG/CRPH Are Told to Surrender

EAOs are treated as armed non-state actors for negotiation.

NUG/CRPH represent a rival **political legitimacy** claim rooted in elections.

Negotiating with them would imply political equivalence.  
Therefore, the language toward them is fundamentally different.

---

## 9️⃣ The Real Disagreement — Starting Points

If you start from:

> “The 2008 Constitution remains the unquestioned legal order”  
→ the military argument looks coherent.

If you start from:

> “The coup broke the constitutional order; legitimacy returns to the people’s vote”  
→ the CRPH/NUG argument becomes coherent.

Both sides are logical **inside their own frame**.

---

## 🔟 Control vs. Consent

Political theory distinguishes:

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| De facto authority | Who controls territory and institutions |
| De jure legitimacy | Who holds the people’s consent |

In stable states, they are the same.  
In Myanmar today, they are not.

That gap is the crisis.

---

## 1️⃣1️⃣ Why This Debate Still Matters

Legitimacy influences:

- International recognition
- Diplomatic engagement
- Sanctions and aid
- Public morale
- The shape of Myanmar’s future constitution

This is not an abstract debate. It shapes the country’s trajectory.

---

## 🧠 Core Insight

This is not about who is “confused.”

It is about whether legitimacy begins from:

- A constitution interpreted by those holding power, or
- A vote expressed by the people before that power was seized.

---

## 🧩 Conclusion

Myanmar’s legitimacy crisis is rooted in a rupture between:

- **Text** and **mandate**  
- **Control** and **consent**

Understanding this reveals why arguments continue without resolution.

And it leads to the central unresolved question:

> **Who truly carries the authority of the Myanmar people today?**
