 When a Leader Blames the People: Decoding the New Year Message



By U Ingar Soe  

17 April 2026



On Myanmar New Year, the country heard a familiar voice deliver an unfamiliar message. In his holiday address, 0 stated that it is difficult to move the country forward with people who have low education levels. The remark, reported by 1, may sound at first like a routine call for improving education.



But placed in today’s context, the message carries a deeper political meaning.



This was not simply a speech about education. It was a speech about responsibility. And more precisely, about where responsibility for the nation’s crisis is being placed.



Not on the state. Not on governance. Not on decisions made by those in power.



But on the people themselves.



---



 A Subtle Shift: From Governance Failure to Public Deficiency



When a leader says the country cannot progress because its people lack education, the focus quietly shifts. The conversation moves away from questions of governance, accountability, and legitimacy, and toward the perceived shortcomings of ordinary citizens.



The message becomes:



> The problem is not how the country is being ruled.  

> The problem is the people who are being ruled.



This is a powerful narrative move. It reframes a political crisis as a social weakness. It transforms a governance issue into a public capability issue.



And once this shift happens, the timeline for change becomes indefinite.



Because improving education takes decades.



---



 Education as a Reason to Delay Politics



Education is always important. No nation disputes this. But in this speech, education is not presented as a parallel priority alongside political reform. It is presented as a precondition.



The implied sequence is clear:



1. First, the people must become educated.

2. Only then can the country move forward.

3. Only then can stability and development occur.



This sequence postpones political questions into an undefined future. If the people are “not ready,” then political participation can be delayed without saying so directly.



The argument becomes:



> “We cannot discuss political solutions yet. The people are not ready.”



In this way, education becomes a political pause button.



---



 Turning Conflict into a Skills Problem



The New Year message also referenced ongoing armed conflicts and urged groups to abandon violence for political dialogue, invoking the 2 as a framework.



But at the same time, military operations and airstrikes have continued in ethnic regions and central Myanmar during the holiday period.



The contradiction is striking.



Conflict is described as something arising from misunderstanding, lack of education, and external influence. But the deeper political causes—loss of trust, demands for representation, and grievances over power—are not addressed.



This turns a political conflict into what sounds like a technical or educational issue.



> If people understood better, they would not resist.



This explanation removes the need to examine why so many are willing to resist despite the risks.



---



 Framing Critics as Obstacles to Progress



Another recurring theme is the warning against “self-interested” individuals and groups. This language creates a moral filter:



- Those who agree are patriotic.

- Those who disagree are selfish.



This framing narrows the space for legitimate dissent. It becomes difficult for alternative viewpoints to be seen as public concern rather than personal agenda.



In such a narrative, criticism itself becomes evidence of disloyalty.



---



 The Psychology of Blame



Blaming citizens for national stagnation has a psychological effect. It encourages people to doubt their own role as political actors. If they are told they are not educated enough, not skilled enough, or not ready enough, they may begin to question whether they deserve a voice in shaping the country’s future.



This creates a passive public.



A public that waits to become “ready.”



A public that is told to improve itself before asking questions of those in power.



---



 Livelihoods Over Legitimacy



The speech also emphasized farming, rural livelihoods, cooperatives, and daily work. These are real concerns for millions of people. But in this context, they serve another purpose: they redirect attention away from political legitimacy and toward economic survival.



The message becomes:



> Focus on working. Focus on stability. Focus on survival. Politics can wait.



When survival takes priority, questions of governance fade into the background.



---



 A Familiar Pattern in Political Messaging



This pattern is not new in political communication. Leaders facing legitimacy crises often shift the conversation from power to productivity, from accountability to development, and from rights to readiness.



The public is asked to prepare itself before it can ask for change.



But this preparation never seems to end.



---



 What Is Being Postponed?



The most important part of the speech is not what it promises, but what it postpones.



It postpones discussions about:



- Political representation  

- Public trust in institutions  

- The reasons behind widespread resistance  

- The role of the state in creating current conditions  



Instead, it asks the public to look inward and ask:



> “Are we educated enough yet?”



---



 The Narrative of “Not Yet”



Combined with earlier speeches about stability, development, and national readiness, this message fits into a broader pattern: the narrative of “not yet.”



- Not yet ready for political solutions  

- Not yet ready for full participation  

- Not yet ready for change  



This can stretch indefinitely.



Because there is no clear measure of when “ready” is achieved.



---



 Listening Beyond the Words



The New Year message uses the language of education, peace, and development. These are positive words. But in political communication, it is important to listen not only to the words, but to the structure of the message.



Who is being asked to change?



Who is being asked to wait?



Who is being asked to improve?



And who is not being asked to answer difficult questions?



---



 The Responsibility Question



Every country values education. But education cannot replace governance. Skills cannot replace accountability. And development cannot replace legitimacy.



When national problems are described as the result of people’s shortcomings, the responsibility of leadership quietly fades from view.



This is the key shift taking place.



---



 Conclusion: A Message About Readiness



This was presented as a New Year message about progress. But at its core, it was a message about readiness.



It tells the public that they must first become better before the country can become better.



It suggests that political solutions must wait for educational solutions.



It frames citizens as the obstacle to progress rather than partners in shaping it.



For observers and citizens alike, the important task is to notice not only what is promised for the future, but what is being deferred in the present.



Because when a leader says the people are not ready, the question that follows is:



Ready for what?



And who decides?
